Welcome to the Homepage of Homepage For Change.

We’re on a mission to give BIPOC students a platform for their art – plus a $2,500 grant to continue making more.

Who

can participate?

This program is open to all Toronto BIPOC students enrolled in high school, post-secondary, or a community arts program. (Don’t worry, we always judge work against those at the same education level.)

What

can I submit &

What

is the selection process?

You can submit any manifestation of your art – whether that’s a photo you took, a photo you took of something you made, or even a Word document containing a short story or poem. Just double-check specs and file types before submitting.

You’re welcome to apply every and any month you like (please do!) but we ask that you only submit one piece of work per month.

Voting will be conducted the first week of every month by a jury within the Juliet agency, judging all the submitted work from the month before. We’ll hum and haw over each piece’s originality, aesthetic, and message when deciding who the recipient of the homepage space – and the $2500 grant – will be. Shortly after the judging process, we’ll contact the recipient of the month via email.

Where

can I submit &

Where

will my work live?

Simply fill out the form provided under the submission section (or just click the button below) and attach your JPEG, PNG, MP4, or Word document containing your piece of work.

If selected, your work will appear on the Juliet homepage for the remainder of the month.

This will give everyone from fellow creatives and artists to producers to directors a chance to see your work. Once the month is up, we’ll catalogue your piece right here on homepageforchange.com, alongside the work of other past recipients.

When

can I submit?

Please make your submission before the month ends, but feel free to enter as many months in a row as you like.

Why

does this program exist?

To give BIPOC artists the platform they deserve – and the means to continue creating their art.

As an advertising agency, we’ve seen firsthand the lack of BIPOC representation in both the work put out and the people who make it. We want to help change that.
